New ASIS International — Security Management June 4, 2026

FBI BAU highlights Behavioral Threat Assessment tactics at ASIS North America

At ASIS’s inaugural North America Conference, the FBI’s Behavioral Threat Assessment Center emphasized practical, human‑centered steps to interrupt pathways to violence. For universities and hospitals, this validates multidisciplinary BTAM programs that pair security with student affairs, HR, legal, and clinical teams. Key takeaways: treat concerning behaviors early; formalize intake and triage of reports; connect individuals to resources; and measure closures and handoffs. Update ASIS International — Security Management June 2, 2026

New benchmarking finds SOC confidence outpaces performance on SLAs

A fresh benchmarking report summarized by Security Management shows most organizations believe their physical security SOCs can detect coordinated threats, yet relatively few consistently meet response-time and alarm-processing SLAs. For hospital and university security directors, the message is to tighten governance around incident intake, cross‑system correlation (access, VMS, duress), and after‑action reporting. Consider formalizing SOC playbooks for panic/duress, weapons detection alerts, and visitor exceptions; define measurable outcomes; and audit escalations with clinical operations or student services. The findings support investments in training analysts, tuning video analytics to reduce false signals, and integrating SOC metrics into enterprise risk dashboards to maintain leadership trust and resource support.

Update ASIS International — Security Management June 1, 2026

From ‘Send Me the Video’ to governance: making video defensible

Security Management’s privacy series outlines how disciplined governance around video evidence protects organizations while enabling investigations. For healthcare and higher ed, this means codifying request pathways, redaction standards, and retention—especially when footage involves patients, minors, or sensitive research areas. The piece urges defining roles (custodian vs. requester), logging releases, and aligning policies with HIPAA/FERPA where applicable. It also encourages privacy impact assessments before deploying new analytics (e.g., weapons detection or face matching) and harmonizing policy with capabilities like masking and audit trails. Adopting these practices reduces reputational risk, supports fair investigations, and helps withstand regulatory or legal scrutiny when incidents escalate or become public.

Update ASIS International — Security Management June 1, 2026

Three privacy tools to build trust into video systems

A companion article profiles three practical approaches—privacy masking, redaction/obfuscation, and workflow controls—that let security teams use video for detection, investigations, and reporting without oversharing personally identifiable information. For hospitals and campuses, these controls are especially relevant around ED waiting areas, student services, clinics, and residence halls where body‑worn cameras and fixed VMS increasingly record sensitive activity. Security programs can pair these tools with policy: default masking for public requests, tiered access rights in the VMS, and redaction SLAs for subpoena response. Aligning tooling with policy helps accelerate case work, limits insider misuse, supports public transparency, and demonstrates ‘privacy-by-design’ to compliance and external stakeholders.
