New Connecticut General Assembly May 27, 2026

Connecticut enacts SB 4, expanding state privacy law and imposing rules on data brokers and DTC genetic testing

Connecticut’s SB 4 (Public Act 26-64) is now law, adding a statewide data-broker registry, an accessible deletion mechanism, and restrictions on certain geolocation and algorithmic pricing practices. It amends the Connecticut Data Privacy Act and sets new compliance duties for direct-to-consumer genetic testing companies, while preserving exemptions for HIPAA-regulated entities and data. For security and privacy teams at health systems, universities with health clinics, and benefits plans, this law affects vendor due diligence, site/app telemetry, consumer rights intake, and cross-border data flows touching non-HIPAA contexts (for example, wellness or genetic services offered directly to consumers). Counsel should map HIPAA vs. state-law scopes, update deletion workflows, and ensure genetic testing vendors can provide verifiable consent, access, and deletion.

New ASIS Security Management June 1, 2026

Video systems: shift from ad hoc access to formal governance

ASIS highlights how modern video, access control, and analytics platforms quickly become evidentiary systems—making privacy governance as important as uptime or coverage. The piece urges security leaders to codify who can view/export footage, how requests are logged and justified, and how retention and redaction are enforced. For hospitals and universities, this helps prevent misuse of patient/student imagery, preserves chain of custody for investigations, and aligns operations with privacy expectations. Action items include building defensible policies for access, retention, and disclosure and ensuring audit trails for every query or export. It’s a timely reminder that technology without governance erodes legitimacy and exposes organizations to reputational and legal risk.

New ASIS Security Management June 1, 2026

Practical privacy controls for surveillance: blur, mask, and redact

A companion ASIS article details three core tools to operationalize privacy in video programs: real-time/recorded blurring, static privacy masking, and post-event redaction. It explains how each control differs, when to apply them (e.g., behavioral health units, public-facing lobbies, or training data sets), and why auditability matters. For healthcare and higher ed, these controls support HIPAA/FERPA-aligned practices, allow monitoring while protecting identities in sensitive spaces, and speed lawful evidence sharing. Security teams can use the framework to update camera design standards, VMS configurations, and SOPs for incident response and public-records or discovery requests, reducing exposure while maintaining situational awareness.

NIST opens SP 800-133r3 draft on cryptographic key generation

NIST released the initial public draft of SP 800-133 Revision 3, updating recommendations for cryptographic key generation and accepting comments through June 16, 2026. Healthcare and university security programs that manage access control credentials, body‑camera storage, VMS archives, or duress/panic systems often rely on cryptography for data at rest and in transit. Teams should review the draft with IT/security architecture to ensure upcoming deployments—especially cloud VMS, AI analytics, and integrated incident platforms—are future‑proof against evolving crypto guidance.
